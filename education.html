Technology in the Classroom

The Evolution of Educational Technology

Technology in the classroom is not a new concept. Before the year 2000, many classrooms had access to radios (1920), overhead projectors (1930), ballpoint pens (1940), headphones (1950), videotapes (1951), photocopiers (1959), handheld calculators and the Scantron System(1972).[6] After 2000, computers and laptops were introduced, which was a major turning point in educational technology. By 2009, 97% of classrooms had at least one computern and 93% of classrooms had internet access.[6] Since then, schools purchased over 23 million devices for classroom use, including iPads and Chromebooks, in just 2013 alone.[7] Today, in 2020, the technology in the classroom extends even beyond just tablets and computers. Teachers are now able to use interactive whiteboards and document cameras in the classroom to show their material.[8]Even games, social media, and online learning tools are being utilized to help students feel engaged with what is being taught to them.[8]

The Benefits of Classroom Technology

One of the first hopes and achievements that technology has brought to the learning environment is better engagements from the students with the materials. Today's students are already using technology, so bringing it into an educational setting is not a difficult transition and actually makes both the student and the teacher more interested in the topic.[4]On top of that, there is an abundance of online educational resources to assist with learning. Open Educational Resources (OER) are defined as, "resources online that are licensed to be freely used, revised, and shared," which the US Department of Education is pushing to be used over textbooks.[7] Khan Academy, YouTube, and Quizlet are just a few examples of other online educational tools that provide lessons and act as effective study tools to help students succeed.[5]Additional examples of online tools that are mentioned in this article include various things like Google Drive, Google Docs, Google Classroom, Google Expeditions, and Slack.
With its sharing abilities, the Google Suite lends itself to allow for easy collaboration between individuals. For students, this means they can work together on assignments and projects outside of the classroom. The Electronic Propinquity Theory explains that people feel closeness when they communicate with others using TMC, especially when they have no other means of communicating. For students, when they leave the classroom, they are limited in how they can interact with others to get work done, specifically for partner or group projects. These limitations make interactions between students via TMC a more enjoyable experience and builds a community that may mimic or exceed the kind of community developed in the classroom.

The creation of online learning and resources completely changed the way students get their information and how some students are able to get their entire education. Some schools offer online programs, while others (known as virtual schools) are completely online. The Florida Virtual High School, the Utah Electronic High School, and the North Carolina Virtual Public High School are just a few schools that are exclusively online.[3] Students also have the option of attending a school that participates in blended learning, which combines in-person and online education. According to some research, Florida had almost 45,000 students in grades 6-12 taking classes online from the Virtual School.[4]These online options especially benefit smaller and rural schools, who can't afford many teachers to teach certain subjects, to be able to use video conferencing and web resources to educate their students.[4]For students with disabilities, having these online options, along with assistive technology, allows for more equality with learning. Students with disabilities are able to learn at their own pace and even get content personalized to help them succeed.[7][8] In this way, the Social Presence Theory can be seen in how online learning gives some students more feelings of being present in their classes because they are able to learn more effectively alongside their peers than they would in person.

Although controversial, there have been mixed findings when it comes to the effects of TMC on test taking. There is some evidence to support the idea that TMC has improved test scores, literacy, and overall outcomes.[8] A specific case involves Maine in the early 2000s. In 2002 and 2003, Maine distributed over 30,000 computers to 7th and 8th graders in public schools.[2] As a result, there was an increase in writing scores on standardized tests. In fact, over 49% of 8th graders reached proficient in writing on the Maine Education Assessment in 2005.[2]. This can be attributed to the fact that "laptops make it easier for students to edit their copy and make changes without getting writerâ€™s cramp [...] (and) those skills translated when the test was taken with pen and paper."[2]

The Cons of Classroom Technology

For the drawbacks of educational technology for the U18 group, excessive screen time and more distractions have created issues for this younger generation. Some studies have discovered that students develop shorter attention spans, which hinders their abilities to complete intricate projects.[8] Another big issue with educational technology has to do with the unequal access to tech resources. Many schools and/or families cannot afford the newer technology that is being pushed, and often the schools do not have enough bandwidth to support the amount of students needing the internet.[7] Additionally, some schools are forced to adopt the new technology, but they don't have any plans for how the technology will be integrated into the existing curriculum.[7] A final example of a drawback with technology in education has to do, once again, with test performance. In 2014 and 2015, online exams became popular with 10 states choosing to use exams from the Partnership for Assessment of Readiness for College and Careers (PARCC) and 18 using exams from the Smarter Balanced Assessment Consortium.[7] It was found that students who took the online PARCC exam did lower, on average, than those who did the traditional exam.[9] This can be related to the efficacy that students feel with technology. If students feel comfortable navigating technology, they are less likely to struggle when it is used in a testing format.

Example

A great example of TMC found in education for the U18 group is through the application Slack. Slack is a cross-platform application made for group collaboration. Within the application, there is the ability to create channels for specific purposes, group messaging, direct messaging, file sharing, voice/video conferencing and more. Many teachers, especially high school teachers, have chosen to use Slack for their classes. With its many channels (literally), it allows for a more engaging experience to keep the students connected to both the class and each other.

One of the biggest ways a technology can keep people from feeling present has to do with time. The traditional way of communication, specifically with teachers, is usually by email. Email is considered an asynchronous form of communication because there is no expectation for an immediate response (nor is there usually one). This prevents people from feeling present with the technology because they can't talk to a person the same way they can face-to-face. Slack, however, acts as more of an instant messenger, which turns it into a platform for synchronous conversation. If a teacher creates a channel like #discussions, students are able to talk to one another in that channel synchronously and in a way that can be comparable to the in-class discussionsâ€”creating more social presence. This aligns heavily with the ideas from the Media Richness Theory, which looks at a mediums ability to allow for shared understanding between people in a given time frame. With its powerful video conferencing and screen sharing ability, students are able to present their ideas and communicate with each other like they would in class if they were giving a presentation or having a discussion in class.

The Future of TMC in Education for U18

With all of the technological advancements, educational technology used in schools for U18 could potentially integrate augmented and/or virtual reality. For students who learn exclusively online, this could change the way they experience their education. It may help increase their feelings of presence by allowing them to feel part of and surrounded by a physical class and classroom.
