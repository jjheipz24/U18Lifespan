Teens and Social Media

What Social Media Does the U18 Group Use?

The current U18 age group has never really experienced a time without social media. Even if they didn't have an account themselves, everyone around them was on and using it. Now, however, the age in which kids get their first social media account is decreasing. In fact, one study determined that about 50% of kids have social media by the age of 12, with the average age being 11 years old.[1] The Pew Research Center conducted a survey on March 7th to April 10th in 2018 that asked about what social media teens aged 13-17 use. Snapchat, YouTube, Instagram, and Facebook were found to be the most popular social media networks that teens use. 35% say they use Snapchat most often, 32% say YouTube, 15% say Instagram, and 10% say Facebook is the platform they use the most.[10] Other popular media that were not listed (or didn't exist yet) are Twitter and TikTok. Lastly, although not considered social media, video games are another popular platform that the U18 group connect with each other on.

The Benefits of Social Media for U18

One large benefit of using social media, not just for U18, is the connectedness that it provides. It helps people stay in contact with in-person ties and can even grow existing friendships. In the study mentioned above, 31% of teens said that they've experienced positive effects from social media; 40% of those said it was attributed to staying in touch and interacting via TMC.[10] In fact, teens actually spend a majority of their time interacting with their friends with TMC than they do in-person (excluding in school). 55% of teens say they text their friends daily, 79% say they instant message their friends every day, and 72% say they spend time with friends on social media.[14] In many cases, TMC is the way many teens receive support from their friends, which helps bring them closer. As discussed in relationship development with TMC, self-disclosures tend to lead to increased feelings of closeness. Teens often use social media to share aspects about their lives with their friends. It was found that 70-83% of teens believed that social media helped them feel closer to their friends' lives and feelings.[14] As a result of gaining insight on what's going on in their friends' lives, teens are able to provide support. In addition to the percentage of teens who learned more about their friends from social media, 68% of teens have had people supporting them through tough times via TMC on social media.[14]

Along a similar vein, social media has helped teens make new friends. Research showed that 57% of 13-17 year old teens who use social media made a new friend online, with only 20% actually meeting those friends offline.[14]The platforms that seem to be used the most in making new friends online are Instagram, Facebook, and video gamesâ€”video games only being used by 36% of participants vs. the 64% for Instagram and/or Facebook.[14] Video games are, however, the primary way that many boys make friends online.[14] These friendships, which are often formed because of common identities in shared online groups, influence people to feel more comfortable expressing themselves. The relative anonymity provided by some online platforms gives teens a feeling of safety and security they wouldn't otherwise have in face-to-face communication. In a survey done, 25% of teens said they felt less shy, 28% said they felt more outgoing, and 20% said they felt more confident (with 53% of those teens identifying as somewhat shy or a lot shy in general).[12]

The Darkside of Social Media

As many parents tell their teenagers, TMC that comes with social media can be extremely harmful, especially with teenagers. With all of the social influencers that flood social media, it can be difficult not to suffer from FOMO (fear of missing out). For teens, 15% of them say that social media makes them have a distorted view of reality and other peoples' lives.[10] As a result, many of the U18 group members feel pressured to look attractive and popular on social media, specifically 40% feel pressured to only post content that makes them look good.[14] This is extremely harmful to identity formation in teens. This "ought" and "ideal" self that teens portray can lead some to feel ashamed of who they really are. Another large issue for teens with TMC via social media is interpersonal conflicts. 68% of teenagers have seen drama on social media, with 15-17 year olds seeing it more frequently than 13-14 year olds (74% vs. 62%).[13] Often this drama is stirred up because of something that happened in person at school, which then gets carried into a digital platform; the reverse happens as well. The lack of cues online increases the chances of miscommunication and is usually the culprit of many disputes between teens. It was found that 26% of teens fought with a friend over something that happened online or because of a text.[13] From these conflicts, because of the power of social media, teens are able to "end" friendships by unfriending, unfollowing, deleting photos, and blocking.[13] Social media has now, in a sense, made the ending of friendships "definitive."

A widely talked about issue and something that often occurs from these internet fights is cyberbullying. About 54% of teens have witnessed cyberbullying online, with only 35% actively stepping in to try to stop it.[15]
